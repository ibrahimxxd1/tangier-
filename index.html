The City's Story
A History Written
in Many Tongues
◆

Tangier is one of the world's great threshold cities. Sitting at the northwestern tip of Africa, where the Strait of Gibraltar narrows to barely 14 kilometers, the city has always been a place of meeting, mixing, and transgression — a seam between continents, cultures, and eras.

Founded as Tingis by the Berber Mauri people, the city was later coveted by the Phoenicians, Carthaginians, Romans, Vandals, Byzantines, Arabs, Portuguese, Spanish, and British. Each left their mark. The layered architecture of the Kasbah tells this story in stone and mortar.

"Tangier is a lot of things to a lot of people. It has been a refuge, a den of spies, a paradise of cheap living, an international scandal, and a place of creative freedom unlike anywhere else on earth."

— The International Zone era, 1923–1956

The most romanticized chapter came in the 20th century. From 1923 to 1956, Tangier was governed by an international committee of eight European nations — a stateless city with no laws around censorship, currency, or customs. It became a magnet for eccentrics, exiles, spies, and artists. The Beat Generation descended: Paul Bowles made it his home for 52 years. William Burroughs wrote Naked Lunch in a medina hotel room. Jack Kerouac and Allen Ginsberg visited. Tennessee Williams came. Even the Rolling Stones.

With Moroccan independence in 1956, the International Zone was absorbed into the new kingdom. The city's wild chapter closed — but Tangier kept its cosmopolitan soul. Today it is Morocco's fastest-growing city, home to a new financial district and the largest port in Africa, yet the medina still feels like a world that time carefully preserved.

◆
5th Century BC
Phoenician Settlement
The Phoenicians establish a trading post at the northern tip of Africa, calling it Tingis — one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world.
◆
33 BC
Roman Tingis
Augustus grants Tingis the status of Roman municipality. It becomes capital of the province of Mauretania Tingitana. Ruins of Roman villas still surface in the modern city.
◆
702 AD
Arab Conquest
Moorish forces under Musa ibn Nusayr take the city. Tangier becomes a launching point for the Islamic conquest of the Iberian Peninsula.
◆
1304
Ibn Battuta is Born
The greatest explorer of the medieval world enters the world here. He will travel 120,000 km over 29 years, visiting 44 modern countries.
◆
1923–1956
The International Zone
Eight European powers jointly administer Tangier. The resulting legal vacuum creates the most freewheeling city on earth — a golden era of art, espionage, and literature.
◆
1956
Moroccan Independence
The International Zone ends as Morocco gains independence under King Mohammed V. The golden age closes, but the legend lives on.
◆
2007–Today
The New Tangier
Tanger-Med becomes Africa's largest container port. A new financial district rises. The city of 700,000 now anchors a grand new vision for Morocco.

A Culinary Portrait
What Tangier
Tastes Like
◆
🫓

Msemen

The folded flatbread of Morocco — layers of semolina dough pressed into a square, griddle-cooked until golden and flaky, served with argan oil and honey. Found at every breakfast table and street corner bakery in the medina, best eaten warm with a glass of tea.

🍲

Harira

The national soup. A thick, warming broth of tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas, lamb, and fragrant spices — cinnamon, ginger, turmeric, saffron — finished with lemon and fresh coriander. During Ramadan it is the meal that breaks the fast. The rest of the year, it is simply magnificent.

🐟

Fresh Seafood

Tangier sits at the confluence of two seas. The fishing boats unload daily at the port: sea bass, red mullet, sole, giant prawns, and the extraordinary boucheron of fresh sardines — the true local speciality. Head to the Port Fish Market for lunch and eat what was swimming that morning.

🥧

Bastilla

The jewel of Moroccan cuisine — a pie of paper-thin warqa pastry layered with spiced pigeon (or chicken), egg, almonds, and dusted with cinnamon and powdered sugar. The savory-sweetness version is extraordinary. In Tangier, it appears on special occasion menus and in the finest restaurants of the medina.

The Art of Mint Tea

In Tangier, mint tea — atay — is not a drink but a ceremony, a philosophy, a relationship. The Moroccan saying goes: the first glass is as gentle as life, the second as strong as love, and the third as bitter as death.

Tea is prepared with theatrical care: the pot raised high to aerate and create foam, the glass filled and poured back three times. To rush it is an insult. To be offered it is a welcome.

  1. Chinese gunpowder green tea is steeped in boiling water, the first steep discarded to rinse the bitterness.
  2. Fresh spearmint — na'na — is packed generously into the teapot along with generous cones of sugar.
  3. The tea is poured from a great height to create the signature foam crown on each glass.
  4. Three rounds are served. The first to visitors, the second to family, the third to contemplation.
Traveller's Notes
Why Visit Tangier
◆

Two Continents in One View

On a clear day from the Kasbah terrace, you can see Spain across the Strait. Tangier is the only city in the world where you can stand at the edge of Africa and see Europe with the naked eye. The geography alone is worth the journey.

Living History Everywhere

Unlike cities that have buried or museumified their past, Tangier wears its history visibly. The medina operates as it has for centuries. Roman columns appear in alleyways. Phoenician anchors rest in the archaeology museum. The past is simply present.

The Literary Pilgrimage

Walk the streets that Paul Bowles, William Burroughs, and Jean Genet walked. Visit the American Legation — the only US National Historic Landmark outside America — which houses a remarkable archive of the International Zone era.

Practical Travel Notes

  • Best season is April–June and September–October. Summer can be crowded, winter is mild and atmospheric.
  • The medina is best explored on foot. Hire a licensed guide for the first morning to orient yourself.
  • Learn a few phrases of Darija (Moroccan Arabic) — even basic greetings transform the local reception.
  • The Petit Socco in the medina is the best place to watch the world go by over a glass of orange juice.
  • Bargaining is expected in the souks. Start at roughly 40% of the asking price and settle in the middle.
  • The train connects Tangier to Casablanca (4hrs) and Marrakech (9h). The new TGV is excellent.
  • Currency is the Moroccan Dirham (MAD). Credit cards work in hotels; carry cash for the medina.
  • Dress modestly in the medina and religious sites. Shoulders and knees covered is the respected norm.
The Tangerine Canon
Souls Shaped by This City
◆
🧭
1304 — 1368

Ibn Battuta

Explorer & Scholar

Born in Tangier, he embarked at age 21 on a journey that would cover 120,000 km across Africa, the Middle East, Central Asia, India, China, and Southeast Asia — more than any explorer before him. His rihla, or travel bible, remains one of the greatest works of world literature.

🎵
1910 — 1999

Paul Bowles

Writer & Composer

The most famous of Tangier's expatriates arrived in 1947, drawn by a postcard from Gertrude Stein. He stayed 52 years. His novel The Sheltering Sky (1949) is a profound meditation on the existential void at the edge of the desert — one of the great American novels, written in Africa.

✒️
1914 — 1997

William Burroughs

Writer

Burroughs spent pivotal years in Tangier's medina, writing what would become Naked Lunch in the Hotel Muniria. He described the city as "a special madness." Kerouac and Ginsberg visited to help him assemble the manuscript. The resulting novel was published to scandal and became a landmark of American literature.

🎨
1869 — 1954

Henri Matisse

Painter

Matisse visited Tangier twice between 1912 and 1913, producing over 24 paintings. The city's extraordinary light, colors, and geometry transformed his palette and compositional approach. The Tangier paintings — studies of the Kasbah, the Zitoun café, the female figure against blue tiles — are among his greatest works.

📖
1910 — 1986

Jean Genet

Playwright & Novelist

The French playwright and lifelong subversive found in Tangier a city that matched his sensibility — radical, empowered, alive with transgression. He spent his last years in the Hotel El-Minzah, completing his final masterwork. He died in a Paris hotel room in 1986 and, by his own request, was buried in the Spanish cemetery in Larache.

📜
c. 124 — 170 AD

Lucius Apuleius

Philosopher & Novelist

The Berber author of The Golden Ass — the only Roman novel to survive completely — was born in the region of Tingitana. Writing in Latin, he produced what is considered the first surviving novel in the Western literary tradition, a picaresque tale of metamorphosis and mystery religions that influenced writers from Boccaccio to Borges.
